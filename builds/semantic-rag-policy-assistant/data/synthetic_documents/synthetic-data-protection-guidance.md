## 1. Data Minimisation

Data minimisation means using only the minimum personal data necessary to achieve a task.
When using AI tools, data minimisation requires:

- Using anonymised or synthetic examples rather than real learner or client data
- Not including names, contact details, or identifying information in AI prompts
- Describing scenarios in general terms rather than referencing specific individuals
- Removing or replacing personal data before using any text with an AI tool

Data minimisation is a core principle under data protection law. Unnecessary sharing of personal
data with any third-party tool — including AI tools — represents a data protection risk and may
constitute a data breach.

## 2. Anonymisation

Before using any real-world information with an AI tool, staff must ensure the information is
fully anonymised. Anonymisation means that no individual could be identified from the information,
directly or indirectly.

Pseudonymisation (replacing names with codes or initials) is not sufficient for use with AI tools.
Full anonymisation or the use of entirely synthetic information is required.

If you are unsure whether information is sufficiently anonymised, do not use it with an AI tool.
Seek guidance from the data protection lead.

## 3. Approved Tools

Only AI tools that have been reviewed and approved by the organisation may be used for work purposes.
The list of approved tools is maintained by the IT and data protection team and reviewed at least
annually.

Staff must not use personal AI tool accounts for work purposes. All AI tool use for work must be
through approved platforms and approved accounts only.

Approved tools have been assessed for:

- Data processing terms and conditions
- Where data is stored and processed geographically
- Data retention and deletion policies
- Security controls and access management
- Compliance with applicable data protection requirements

Using an unapproved tool for work purposes — even informally — creates data protection risk and
may breach this policy.

## 4. Access Control

Access to AI tools used for work purposes is managed by the IT team. Staff must not share
login credentials for approved AI tools. Access rights should be reviewed when a member of staff
changes role or leaves the organisation.

AI-generated outputs used in official documents or communications may be subject to data subject
access requests and should be handled accordingly. Staff should not treat AI tool conversation
histories as an approved record-keeping system.

## 5. Retention

AI-generated content that is incorporated into official documents or communications must be retained
in line with the organisation's records retention schedule.

Informal AI-assisted drafts that are discarded or not incorporated into official documents do not
require separate retention. Staff should not retain AI-generated content containing personal data
beyond its immediate working purpose.

Approved AI tools with data retention features should be configured in line with the organisation's
retention schedule where possible.

## 6. Privacy Review and DPIA

Any new or significantly changed use of AI tools that involves personal data should be assessed for
data protection impact before deployment. This is a placeholder section — a real organisation would
define the DPIA trigger criteria and process here.

As a general principle: if a new AI use case involves personal data, a privacy review should be
completed before the tool or use case goes live. A Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) may
be required for high-risk processing activities. Consult the data protection lead.

## 7. Incident Reporting

Any data protection incident involving AI tools must be reported immediately through the incident
reporting pathway. Incidents include:

- Accidental entry of personal data into an AI tool
- Suspected data breach via an AI tool or AI-generated output
- Unexpected sharing of personal data in AI-generated content
- Loss of control over personal data processed through an AI tool

Staff must report incidents to the data protection lead as soon as possible after discovery.
Significant incidents may require notification to the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO)
within 72 hours. Delay in reporting increases organisational risk.
