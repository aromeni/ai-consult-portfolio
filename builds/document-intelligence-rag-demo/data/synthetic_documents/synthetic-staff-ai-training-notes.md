## 1. Safe Prompting

A prompt is the instruction you give to an AI tool. Safe prompting means writing clear, specific
instructions using only anonymised, synthetic, or general information.

**Principles of safe prompting:**

- Use general descriptions, not specific individuals or real-sounding details
- Replace names and identifying details with fictional alternatives before writing a prompt
- State the format you want the output in (bullet points, paragraph, table, numbered list)
- Be specific about the purpose — AI tools respond better to focused, contextual prompts
- Avoid prompts that require the AI to produce outputs you cannot verify or review

**Example of a safe prompt:**

> "Write a three-paragraph introduction to a half-day AI awareness session for staff who are
> new to AI tools. The audience is non-technical. Keep the tone friendly and practical."

**Example of an unsafe prompt:**

> "A staff member at my organisation submitted a complaint about [name]. Help me respond."

The difference is simple: the safe prompt uses general, fictional content. The unsafe prompt
involves a real individual and a sensitive HR matter.

## 2. Checking Outputs

All AI-generated outputs must be carefully checked before use. Human review is not optional.
Checking AI-generated content means:

- Reading the full output carefully, not just skimming
- Verifying any factual claims, statistics, or references against a reliable source
- Checking the content is appropriate for the intended audience and context
- Removing or correcting any inaccurate, misleading, or inappropriate content before use
- Confirming that no personal data has been inadvertently included in the output

Staff are accountable for AI-generated content they use or share. Using an unchecked output
is a professional risk regardless of who or what produced it.

## 3. Hallucination Risk

AI tools can produce outputs that appear confident and well-written but are factually incorrect.
This is sometimes called hallucination. AI tools may:

- Invent statistics, case references, or quotes that do not exist
- Describe policies, legislation, or guidance inaccurately
- Produce plausible-sounding but incorrect summaries of real documents
- Generate content that contradicts established guidance or professional standards
- Cite sources that do not exist or are not accessible

Hallucination risk is highest when the AI tool is asked about specific facts, figures, named
legislation, or real documents. Staff must never cite AI-generated content as a source without
independently verifying it through a reliable reference.

## 4. Bias and Fairness

AI tools are trained on large datasets that may reflect historical biases. AI-generated content
can reproduce or reinforce bias in areas including:

- Gender, race, ethnicity, and cultural representation in examples and scenarios
- Assumptions about learner ability, background, or circumstances
- Stereotyping in case studies, role descriptions, or illustrations
- Uneven quality and accuracy across different languages, communities, and contexts

Staff should review AI-generated content for fairness and bias before use. This is especially
important when creating training materials, assessments, or learner-facing communications.
If in doubt about whether content is fair and representative, ask a colleague to review.

## 5. Copyright Awareness

AI tools generate content based on training data that may include copyrighted material.
Staff should be aware that:

- AI-generated text may unintentionally reproduce copyrighted phrases, passages, or structures
- AI tools must not be asked to reproduce substantial extracts from published books, articles,
  or other protected works
- AI-generated images, code, or other content may be subject to intellectual property disputes
- Staff should not represent AI-generated content as entirely their own original work in contexts
  where originality matters (e.g., academic or professional submissions)

When in doubt about intellectual property rights, seek guidance before publishing, distributing,
or submitting AI-generated content.

## 6. When to Ask a Manager

Staff must always ask a manager or seek guidance before:

- Using an AI tool for a new purpose not covered by existing guidance or approved use cases
- Using an AI tool to support a decision that affects a learner's progress, welfare, or rights
- Sharing AI-generated content externally with parents, carers, employers, or partners
- Using AI tools to handle a complaint, grievance, or sensitive HR matter
- Using AI tools in a context involving regulated information or professional accountability
- Using AI tools to generate content that will be presented as original research or assessment

When in doubt, do not proceed with AI use until you have sought guidance. Pausing to check is
always the appropriate response when the safe boundary is unclear.

## 7. Examples of Safe Use in the Classroom and Administration

### Safe classroom uses (anonymised content only)

- Generating draft lesson plan outlines for a topic area using fictional scenarios
- Creating multiple-choice question banks using hypothetical characters and situations
- Producing first drafts of plain-English learner handouts for human review
- Summarising publicly available policy documents for staff training resources
- Generating ideas for discussion activities, group exercises, or role-play scenarios
- Creating accessible language versions of general information for learner support

### Safe admin uses (anonymised content only)

- Drafting routine correspondence templates for general communication purposes
- Summarising publicly available research, reports, or sector guidance
- Proofreading and improving the clarity of non-personal documents
- Generating meeting agenda templates, action log formats, and standard forms
- Creating first-draft organisational policies for qualified human review

### Not safe (regardless of approval status)

- Processing any learner personal data, assessment records, or attendance information
- Drafting responses to complaints, grievances, or appeals involving named individuals
- Handling, recording, or seeking guidance on safeguarding information or welfare concerns
- Generating or summarising HR, disciplinary, or confidential staff records
- Producing content that will be acted on without human review
