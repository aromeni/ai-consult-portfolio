## 1. Overview

Safeguarding is a human responsibility. AI tools play no role in safeguarding decision-making
at BrightPath Skills Training. This guidance sets out the boundaries that all staff must understand
before using AI tools in any aspect of their work.

The key principle is simple: **if it involves a real person's welfare, safety, or vulnerability,
it must not be entered into an AI tool.**

## 2. Safeguarding Information Must Not Be Entered into AI Tools

Staff must never enter safeguarding information into any AI tool. Safeguarding information includes:

- Disclosures made by learners, parents, carers, or others about abuse, neglect, or exploitation
- Concerns about a learner's welfare or safety
- Information about a learner's vulnerability, protected characteristics, or personal circumstances
  relevant to safeguarding
- Child protection referrals, case notes, or records of any kind
- Information about contact with external agencies (police, social services, health services)
- Names, descriptions, or details of any individual involved in a safeguarding matter

This boundary applies regardless of whether the AI tool is approved for other work purposes.
No exception exists for safeguarding information under any circumstances.

## 3. Staff Must Escalate to the Safeguarding Lead

When a safeguarding concern arises, staff must follow the agreed escalation pathway:

1. Do not attempt to handle the matter alone or seek guidance from an AI tool
2. Do not use an AI tool to draft a response, record the concern, or investigate the situation
3. Contact the Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL) immediately — in person or by phone
4. Record the concern in the agreed safeguarding recording system only, following DSL instructions
5. Follow the DSL's instructions at all times without deviation

The escalation pathway is a human-led process. AI tools are not part of this pathway at any stage.

## 4. AI Cannot Make Safeguarding Decisions

AI tools cannot assess risk, judge the credibility of a disclosure, determine whether a referral
is required, or make any safeguarding decision. These judgements require:

- Human expertise and professional training
- Knowledge of the individual and their context
- Accountability to a recognised legal and ethical framework
- Documented decision-making that can be scrutinised

Staff who use AI tools to seek guidance on active safeguarding situations are in breach of this
policy and create serious risk of harm. Any safeguarding question that arises in practice must
be directed to the DSL — not to an AI tool.

## 5. Examples of Safe and Unsafe Prompts

### Safe prompts (anonymised, hypothetical, training context only)

These examples use entirely fictional content and are suitable for training material development:

- "Write a scenario for a staff training exercise about recognising signs of neglect — use a
  completely fictional character with no connection to any real person."
- "What are the general principles of safer recruitment? Give me a plain-English overview for
  a staff induction handout."
- "Summarise the general principles of the UK safeguarding framework for a staff training
  resource — do not include real case details."

### Unsafe prompts (never use — these breach this policy)

- "A learner told me that [name] has been [description of abuse]. What should I do?"
- "Write a safeguarding referral for a learner who disclosed [details of disclosure]."
- "I have a concern about a vulnerable learner. Here are the details: [details]. Is this
  serious enough to report?"
- "Summarise this safeguarding meeting note: [content with identifying details]."
- "A parent contacted me to say their child is being [description]. Can you help me respond?"

The key distinction is clear: safe prompts use fictional or general information. Unsafe prompts
involve real individuals, real situations, or real disclosures.

## 6. Human-Led Decision-Making

All decisions that affect the welfare, safety, or rights of learners must be made by a responsible,
accountable human professional. AI tools can support administrative tasks and general information
retrieval, but they cannot replace human judgement in safeguarding or welfare decisions.

Staff are personally responsible for the outputs they use and share. Using AI-generated output in
a safeguarding context — without appropriate human review and professional oversight — represents
a serious professional and organisational risk that may cause harm.
